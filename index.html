🌋 Tantrum Protocol

Breathe. You've got this. Follow these steps.

1
🛑 STOP — Check Yourself First
Take one deep breath. If you're escalated, they'll feel it. Your calm IS the intervention. Lower your body — kneel or sit on the floor so you're at their level.
2
🛡️ SAFETY — Remove Danger, Not the Child
Move hard/sharp objects away. If they're hitting or throwing, gently block without restraining. Don't pick them up unless they're in danger — it can escalate.
3
🗣️ NAME IT — Be Their Words
At 2.5 they don't have the language for what they feel. You say it for them in a calm, low voice:
"You're really mad right now. You wanted _____ and you can't have it. That's so frustrating."
4
🧱 HOLD THE BOUNDARY — Don't Negotiate
Say the boundary ONCE, then stop repeating it. Repeating = negotiating. They heard you the first time — they just don't like it.
"I won't let you hit. I'm going to keep you safe."
5
🫂 STAY CLOSE — Be a Lighthouse
Don't walk away, don't isolate them. Sit nearby and be still. They need to know you won't abandon them even at their worst. If they push you away, say:
"I'm right here. I'll be here when you're ready."
6
⏳ WAIT — Let the Storm Pass
A tantrum has a peak and a come-down. Don't try to fix it during the peak — there is no reasoning with a flooded brain. Average tantrum: 2–5 minutes. It WILL end.
7
💛 RECONNECT — After the Storm
When it's over, don't lecture. Don't say "are you done?" Open your arms and wait. When they come to you:
"That was really big. I'm proud of you for calming down. I love you no matter what."
🧠 Remember: This Is Not Misbehavior At 2.5, tantrums are a neurological event — their prefrontal cortex is years away from managing big emotions. You are literally teaching their brain how to regulate by staying calm yourself. You are their safe place. That's why they melt down with YOU.
